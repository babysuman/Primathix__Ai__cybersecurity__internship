## \# Dark web :

###### The dark web is a hidden layer of the internet that is intentionally unindexed by standard search engines. It requires specialized software, such as the Tor Browser, to access. It uses encrypted "onion routing" to hide users' identities and locations. While it supports privacy, it is also a hub for illicit marketplaces and cybercrime.



##### Understanding the Dark Web:

###### The internet is divided into three main layers:

1. ###### Surface Web: The standard websites indexed by regular search engines like Google or Bing.
2. ###### Deep Web: Any content hidden behind paywalls, passwords, or registration, such as private databases, online banking, and email accounts.
3. ###### Dark Web: A small, encrypted subset of the deep web. It relies on overlay networks (or darknets) to provide total anonymity.





##### How It Works:

###### Instead of connecting directly to a website server as you do on the surface web, dark web networks route your connection through a series of randomly selected, volunteer-run servers. This layered encryption (referred to as garlic or onion routing) obscures your IP address and location.

###### Dark web sites are not found via .com or .org domains; instead, they use complex .onion URLs ending with long, cryptographic strings of random letters and numbers.



##### Usage and Risks:

###### Because of its extreme anonymity, the dark web has a complex reputation.

1. Privacy and Activism: It is used globally by journalists, whistleblowers, and political dissidents in oppressive regimes to communicate securely and bypass surveillance or censorship.

   ---
2. Cybercrime and Markets: It is famously associated with illicit marketplaces, hacking forums, the trade of stolen credentials, and malware distribution.

   ---
3. ###### Security: While accessing the dark web is generally legal in most places, it carries inherent cybersecurity risks. Users face significant exposure to malware, scams, and threat actors who trade compromised information.



#### \# Tor browser:

###### Tor Browser is a free, privacy-focused web browser that routes your internet traffic through a volunteer-operated network to hide your location, identity, and browsing habits. It is commonly used by journalists, activists, and individuals seeking strict online anonymity or access to heavily censored content.



##### How It Works: Onion Routing

###### &#x20;Tor (which stands for The Onion Router) gets its name from its unique     

###### &#x20;method of securing data:

1. Multi-Layered Encryption: When you visit a website using Tor Browser, your data is encrypted multiple times—like the layers of an onion.

   ---
2. Random Relays (Nodes): Your traffic is passed through at least three random servers (entry, middle, and exit nodes) across the global Tor network.

   ---
3. ###### Decryption: Each node peels away just one layer of encryption to reveal only the next stop for the data. No single server ever knows both your identity (IP address) and your final destination.
